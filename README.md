# Microbiome diversity among drought-tolerant peanut genotypes

This repository provides supporting information and the codes for the following manuscript.

### Unique patterns of microbial diversity in bulk soil associated with drought-tolerant and drought-susceptible peanut cultivars in Alabama

Zoe J. Little<sup>1</sup>, Deepak Shantharaj<sup>1</sup>, Alvaro Saez Sanz<sup>2</sup>, Charles Chen<sup>2</sup>, and Neha Potnis<sup>1</sup>

<sup>1</sup> Department of Entomology and Plant Pathology, Auburn University, Auburn, Alabama AL 36849

<sup>2</sup> Department of Crop, Soil and Environmental Sciences, Auburn University, Auburn, Alabama AL 36849

## Abstract

Drought has been identified as a dominant threat to the peanut industry. As the severity and duration of droughts are expected to increase alongside rapidly changing environmental conditions, new strategies are being developed to increase crop resilience to drought and other abiotic stressors. The host-associated microbiome acts as an extended layer of plant immunity by providing tolerance towards biotic and abiotic stressors, which has led to interest in how host-associated microbiome might be shaped by drought tolerant peanut cultivars to provide unique traits of improved plant growth and stress resistance. To study potential baseline patterns of microbiome assembly across resistant cultivars of differing drought tolerance mechanisms, we assessed microbial DNA samples from bulk soil, rhizosphere, and root endosphere compartments of six control group peanut cultivars with unique physiological mechanisms of water tolerance (water-saver, water-spender, and sensitive host phenotypes) from Auburn University’s peanut breeding program. Results showed that, when grouped by phenotype, alpha diversity of bacterial and fungal communities had highest differences among the bulk soil compartments. Significant changes in fungal diversity among host phenotype categories were observed among the rhizosphere and root endosphere compartments, with uniquely enriched fungal genera seen in water spender and water saver phenotype in bulk soil. This relationship between bacterial and fungal assembly patterns suggests a potential host-specific rhizosphere effect that could be influenced by changes in root exudate composition or abiotic soil conditions associated with the host. Further work is needed to determine if there is a link between microbial enrichment patterns and the physiological expression of drought tolerance among the three categories, or if these associations directly contribute to the host’s ability to mount an effective defense response against drought stress.
